Explore the data

Our study utilized two datasets. The first was compiled by Christian Bjørnskov and Martin Rode, two economists who study political institutions and how governments change over time. Their goal was to create a resource for researchers studying political transitions, an area they felt had not been adequately covered by existing data. The dataset collects information on 192 sovereign countries and 16 self-governing territories annually from 1950 to 2020 on each country’s regime type, democratic status, elections, leadership, suffrage, transfers of power, and colonial history. In total, it includes 14,768 country-year observations across 43 variables.

To build this dataset, the authors expanded on an existing collection of political data by recording key features of each country’s government, such as whether elections took place, whether those elections were considered free and fair, and whether leadership changed hands peacefully. Records of coups were verified across newspaper archives, encyclopedias, and other databases to ensure accuracy. For our analysis, this dataset is especially useful because it gives us a reliable way to classify each country as democratic or not for any given year, while also providing the broader political context needed to understand how and why governments change over time.

The second dataset comes from the World Bank, which tracks global progress on the UN’s 17 Sustainable Development Goals, a set of goals aimed at making the world more equitable and sustainable across areas like poverty, education, and gender equality. The 5th goal focuses specifically on gender equality, and one of its key indicators is the percentage of parliamentary seats held by women in each country. This figure is compiled by the World Bank using data from the Inter-Parliamentary Union, an organization that collects official seat counts directly from legislative bodies around the world. It is available annually for most countries from 1997 to 2020, with coverage becoming more consistent in the later years of that range.

The proportion of female presidents is higher for democratic countries than non-democratic countries. The proportions of male presidents are similar across democratic and non-democratic countries, but the proportion of male presidents for democratic countries is slightly lower. Non-democratic countries’ female presidents are, on average, about 13.7 years older than male presidents. For democratic countries, that difference is much lower, at 2.81 years.

The proportion of male monarchs in non-democratic countries is 84.1 percentage points higher than that of female monarchs. However, in democratic countries, the proportions of male and female monarchs are very similar (47.8% and 52.2%, respectively). Monarchs of both gender are much younger on average than presidents, around 30-35 years old. Non-democratic countries seem to have younger monarchs than democratic countries.

Next, we created a ribbon plot to highlight the differences in age between average accession ages of presidents and monarchs, by gender. We omitted years before 1980, as there were no female presidents before then, so we did not have any values for the average female presidents’ accession age.

From 1980 to 2020, gender differences in the age at which leaders take office vary between presidents and monarchs. Among presidents, the gap is small and relatively stable, with men only slightly older than women at the time they take office. Both groups also show a gradual increase in age over time, suggesting that gender plays little role in when presidents begin their terms. In contrast, monarchs show a much larger and growing gap. Male monarchs tend to take the throne at older ages, while female monarchs are generally younger, with their ages even decreasing in more recent years. Overall, gender disparities in age at taking office are minimal in elected systems but much more pronounced in monarchical ones, where succession patterns create stronger divides.

Democracies tend to have slightly higher representation of women in national parliaments compared to non-democracies. The median percentage is higher for democracies (15.8%) than for non-democracies (14%), and the mean is also somewhat higher (17.7% vs. 16%).

However, the distributions are fairly similar overall, with substantial overlap between the two groups’ boxplots. Both democracies and non-democracies show wide variability and several outliers. Non-democratic countries seem to have more outliers, including some at higher values, with two outliers exceeding 60%.

Fitting a model

We ran a t-test to compare the means of the proportion of national parliament seats held by women between different democracy statuses.

This suggests that the true difference in means is not zero.
