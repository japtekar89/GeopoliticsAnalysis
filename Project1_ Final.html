Explore the data

Our study utilized two datasets. The first was compiled by Christian Bjørnskov and Martin Rode, two economists who study political institutions and how governments change over time. Their goal was to create a resource for researchers studying political transitions, an area they felt had not been adequately covered by existing data. The dataset collects information on 192 sovereign countries and 16 self-governing territories annually from 1950 to 2020 on each country’s regime type, democratic status, elections, leadership, suffrage, transfers of power, and colonial history. In total, it includes 14,768 country-year observations across 43 variables.

To build this dataset, the authors expanded on an existing collection of political data by recording key features of each country’s government, such as whether elections took place, whether those elections were considered free and fair, and whether leadership changed hands peacefully. Records of coups were verified across newspaper archives, encyclopedias, and other databases to ensure accuracy. For our analysis, this dataset is especially useful because it gives us a reliable way to classify each country as democratic or not for any given year, while also providing the broader political context needed to understand how and why governments change over time.

The second dataset comes from the World Bank, which tracks global progress on the UN’s 17 Sustainable Development Goals, a set of goals aimed at making the world more equitable and sustainable across areas like poverty, education, and gender equality. The 5th goal focuses specifically on gender equality, and one of its key indicators is the percentage of parliamentary seats held by women in each country. This figure is compiled by the World Bank using data from the Inter-Parliamentary Union, an organization that collects official seat counts directly from legislative bodies around the world. It is available annually for most countries from 1997 to 2020, with coverage becoming more consistent in the later years of that range.

We began by looking at the percentages of women’s parliamentary seats in democratic and non-democratic countries. On average, democracies tend to have slightly higher representation, with a median of 15.8% compared to 14.0% in non-democratic countries, and a mean of 17.7% versus 16.0%. That said, the two groups look fairly similar overall, with a lot of overlap between them and wide variation within each group. Non-democratic countries do show a few unusually high values, including two countries where women held more than 60% of parliamentary seats.

Female presidents are more common in democratic countries than in non-democratic ones, while the share of male presidents stays relatively consistent regardless of democracy status. The most notable finding in the accession age table is that female presidents in non-democratic countries take office at nearly 14 years older on average than male presidents. In democratic countries, that difference is much smaller, at under 3 years.

For monarchs, the pattern is quite different. In non-democratic countries, male monarchs outnumber female monarchs by more than 84 percentage points, while in democratic countries the split is much closer to even. Monarchs also tend to come to power younger than presidents, usually somewhere between 30 and 35 years old, and this is especially true in non-democratic countries.

To get a clearer sense of how these age gaps have shifted over time, we constructed a ribbon plot showing the difference between average accession ages for male and female presidents and monarchs by decade. Because there were no recorded female presidents before 1980, we limited this portion of the analysis to years from 1980 onward.

From 1980 to 2020, gender differences in the age at which leaders take office vary between presidents and monarchs. Among presidents, the gap is small and relatively stable, with men only slightly older than women at the time they take office. Both groups also show a gradual increase in age over time. In contrast, monarchs show a much larger and growing gap. Male monarchs tend to take the throne at older ages, while female monarchs are generally younger, with their ages even decreasing in more recent years. Overall, gender disparities in age at taking office are minimal in presidential systems but much more pronounced in monarchical ones.

This table aggregates democratic transitions by decade, offering a cleaner view of long-term trends in global democratization from 1950 to 2020. The proportion of democracies worldwide grew from 29.0% in the 1950s to a peak of 63.6% in the 2010s, representing more than a doubling over seven decades. The 1990s stand out as the most transformative decade, with 39 transitions into democracy and only 11 out, reflecting the wave of democratization that followed the end of the Cold War. In contrast, the 1970s were the most stagnant period, with transitions in and out nearly balanced at 16 and 15 respectively and virtually no net change in the global proportion. The 2020 row, which represents only a single year, shows 0 transitions in and 2 out, hinting at the modest democratic backsliding visible at the tail end of the dataset.

The correlation heatmap does not suggest that transitions into or out of democracies influence the proportion of democratic countries across the world. The values of -0.05 and -0.06 indicate that fluctuations in democratic transitions are not associated with changes in the proportion of democratic countries.

We also wanted to identify which countries had the most transitions into and out of democracy.

Code 
top10_countries <- function(data) {
  
  data <- data |>
    arrange(country_name, year) |>
    group_by(country_name) |>
    mutate(
      prev_year = lag(is_democracy),
      to_democracy = ifelse(is_democracy == 1 & prev_year == 0, 1, 0),
      from_democracy = ifelse(is_democracy == 0 & prev_year == 1, 1, 0)
    ) |>
    ungroup()
  
  country_table <- data |>
    group_by(country_name) |>
    summarize(
      transitions_in = sum(to_democracy, na.rm = TRUE),
      transitions_out = sum(from_democracy, na.rm = TRUE),
      total_transitions = transitions_in + transitions_out,
      .groups = "drop") |>
    arrange(desc(total_transitions)) |>
     slice_head(n = 10) 
  
  return(country_table)
}
