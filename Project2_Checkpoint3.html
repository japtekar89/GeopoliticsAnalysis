Honduras Trends
  • Honduras as a Representative Case
  • Women in Parliament Trends
  • Global Trends

Honduras had 6 transitions in and out of democracy. The difference from the average proportion of women in parliament from before 2005 and after 2020 was 0.5212923, which is pretty close to the average as well. So, it both represents the global trend for women in parliament pretty well, but acts as an outlier for the number of transitions in and out of democracy.

We found data about Honduras through the World Bank database, which includes data about over 1,300 categories about Honduras, including information about population breakdowns, social issues, climate change, and economic-related things. The data was collected from 1960–2025, and we cleaned it to have each row represent one year.

The female population in Honduras remains relatively constant over time. However, the percentage of women in parliament fluctuates throughout the years. In 1997, only about 9% of seats in parliament were occupied by women, and in 2002, this percentage dropped to around 5.5%. 2005 saw a significant increase of 23% in the percentage of women in parliament. This percentage has varied since then, however the percentage of women in parliament seems to be on the rise, with almost 30% of seats being held by women in 2025.

South America has a large concentration of female leaders. There are also a few European, African, and Asian countries with either female monarchs or presidents. Generally, it seems that smaller territories and countries, such as the Cayman Islands and Nepal, have female leaders more than larger countries. The map also reveals that countries with female leaders don’t necessarily have a large proportion of women in parliament. For example, Liberia, South Korea, and Greece are some countries who once had female leaders, but have low proportions of women in parliament.

Overall, our analysis shows that democracy is related to gender representation and political stability, but not always in simple ways. Democratic countries had slightly higher average proportions of women in parliament than non-democratic countries, and the t-test showed that this difference was statistically significant. However, the actual difference in means was fairly small, suggesting that democracy alone does not guarantee equal representation. Female presidents became more common over time, but male presidents still made up the large majority of presidential leaders across every decade. Accession-age gaps were smaller and more stable among presidents, while monarchs showed larger differences by gender.

Our analysis of democratic transitions also showed that global democratization has been unpredictable. The percentage of democratic countries increased substantially from 1950 to 2020, especially around the late 1980s and early 1990s, but this growth was not steady. Countries continued to move both into and out of democracy over time, showing that democratization includes both progress and reversal. The country-level transition tables suggest that even a small number of regime changes can reflect meaningful political instability.

Taken together, these findings show that becoming a democratic country can be associated with greater gender representation and broader political change, but maintaining democratic rule and achieving gender equality remain ongoing challenges. Democracy may create more opportunities for representation, but the persistence of gender gaps and repeated regime transitions show that political progress is not automatic or permanent.
